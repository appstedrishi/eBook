Calvin cycle

Definition of a Calvin cycle

The second of two major stages in photosynthesis (following the light reactions), involving fixation of atmospheric CO2 and reduction of the fixed carbon into carbohydrate.

Calvin cycle is a type of: anabolic pathway and cyclic reaction.

Properties of a Calvin cycle

  • Free energy change
    • inherent to all endergonic reactions
      • positive with respect to an exergonic reaction

Participants of a Calvin cycle

Calvin cycle — ATP, carbon dioxide, an NADPH and ribulose bisphosphate are converted in a cell to an ADP, an NADP plus, a phosphate ion and sugar. This process requires free-energy.

Steps of Calvin cycle

  1. Carbon fixation — ATP, carbon dioxide and ribulose bisphosphate are converted in a cell resulting in a 3 phosphoglycerate. This process requires activation energy.
  2. Reduction In Calvin cycle consumes ATP and a 3 phosphoglycerate and produces glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate
  3. Regeneration of ribulose bisphosphate consumes 3 ATP and 5 glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate and produces 3 ADPs and 3 ribulose bisphosphate

Types of Calvin cycles

  • eukaryotic Calvin cycle
