Fermentation

fermentation

A catabolic process that makes a limited amount of ATP from glucose without an electron transport chain and that produces a characteristic end product, such as ethyl alcohol or lactic acid.

Definition of a fermentation

A catabolic process that makes a limited amount of ATP from glucose without an electron transport chain and that produces a characteristic end product, such as ethyl alcohol or lactic acid.

Fermentation is a type of: catabolic pathway .

Properties of a fermentation

  • Free energy change
    • inherent to all exergonic reactions
      • negative with respect to an endergonic reaction

Participants of a fermentation

Fermentation — glucose is converted in a cell to at least 2 organic molecules (2 ATP, an NADH). This process transforms activation energy to free-energy.

Steps of fermentation

  • Glycolysis — 2 ADPs, glucose and 2 NAD plus are converted in a cell to 2 ATP, 2 NADHs, 2 pyruvate and a water molecule in cytosol. This process transforms activation energy to free-energy.
  • Redox reaction consumes an NADH and an organic molecule and produces NAD plus

Types of fermentations

  • lactic acid fermentation
  • alcohol fermentation

Energy-Yielding Metabolic Pathways Energy-yielding reactions can be grouped into five metabolic pathways: glycolysis, pyruvate oxidation, the citric acid cycle, the respiratory chain/ATP synthesis, and fermentation. The three lower pathways occur only in the presence of O 2 and are collectively referred to as cellular respiration. When O 2 is unavailable, glycolysis is followed by fermentation.

Cellular Respiration Yields More Energy Than Fermentation Electron carriers are reduced in pyruvate oxidation and the citric acid cycle, then oxidized by the respiratory chain. These reactions produce ATP via chemiosmosis.
Figure 9.11
Fermentation Glycolysis produces pyruvate, ATP, and NADH from glucose. Lactic acid fermentation uses NADH as a reducing agent to reduce pyruvate to lactate, thus regenerating NAD + to keep glycolysis operating. In alcoholic fermentation, pyruvate from glycolysis is converted into acetaldehyde, and CO 2 is released. NADH from glycolysis is used to reduce acetaldehyde to ethanol, thus regenerating NAD + to keep glycolysis operating.
